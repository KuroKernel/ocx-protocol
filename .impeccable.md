# Design Context — OCX Protocol website

## Users

Three audiences in priority order:

1. **Enterprise buyers in regulated industries** (Indian and global banks, insurers, healthcare AI, audit firms). These people sign ₹25 lakh – ₹3 Cr / year contracts. They land on the site after hearing about OCX from a colleague, an analyst, or a paper. They have eight seconds to decide whether OCX is "real" or "another startup pitch." If the site signals "startup template," they leave.

2. **Cryptography researchers and academic readers**. They came from the IACR ePrint listing or a paper citation. They want to read the spec, see the empirical results, verify the receipts. They are skeptical by default and respect rigor. The site needs to make the technical content immediately accessible — links to PDF, schema table, verifier ABI, run commands.

3. **Open-source developers** building integrations. They want a GitHub link, install command, working verifier, and clear API surface.

## Brand personality

Three concrete words: **verifiable, load-bearing, quiet.**

- *Verifiable* — every claim points at evidence. No adjectives, no superlatives. Numbers come from committed files. The reader can re-execute and confirm.
- *Load-bearing* — this is infrastructure that other systems will depend on. The visual register should feel structurally sound, not stylish. Compare: a steel I-beam, an RFC document, a notarised seal.
- *Quiet* — Bitcoin paper register. Confident enough to not need decoration. Serious infrastructure protocols (TLS, JWT, OAuth, OAuth, CBOR itself) succeed by being boring to adopt. The site should feel like reading a precise spec, not a marketing deck.

## Aesthetic direction

**Pure black on pure white. No cream. No blue. No indigo. No gradients. No accent colors.**

Greyscale only, perfectly achromatic, OKLCH-defined. Two background tones (paper and a barely-tinted ash for section variation). Type does the work; whitespace does the work; the protocol's actual artifacts (CBOR receipts, equations, hex hashes, comparison tables) are the visual.

Reference sites that capture the right register:
- **Stripe docs (`docs.stripe.com`)** — typographic discipline, monospace as design language, technical artifacts as the visual
- **Tailscale.com** — restrained but with technical diagrams that explain the protocol
- **Linear.app** — varies section heights, mixes content types without breaking the system
- **Cloudflare Workers product pages** — interweaves code, diagrams, and prose

Anti-references (what this site must NOT look like):
- Generic "modern startup landing page" with 4-up stats grid, three identical feature cards, dark hero with gradient buttons
- Glassmorphism, neumorphism, anything published in a 2024 design trend listicle
- Any site with rounded-corner icons above headings
- Cream / warm beige backgrounds — explicitly rejected by the user
- Inter typeface — banned by the impeccable skill for being the AI design monoculture
- Any blue or indigo — explicitly rejected by the user

The user views the site on a **Samsung OLED G9** (49-inch ultrawide). Whitespace must be generous on a normal screen so it doesn't feel cramped on the ultrawide. Default to MORE whitespace than feels normal.

## Design principles

1. **The artifact IS the design.** Show the actual CBOR receipt, the actual hex hash, the actual equation, the actual schema. These are the visual elements. Sections without artifacts are scaffolding and should be tighter, not pretend-designed.

2. **Numbers without preachy framing.** Don't title a section "Numbers, not adjectives" — just label it "Evidence" and let the numbers speak. Bitcoin paper doesn't editorialise about itself.

3. **Vary vertical rhythm deliberately.** Hero gets near-full-viewport. Receipt anatomy section gets twice the breathing room of the comparison table. Don't repeat `py-24 md:py-32` everywhere.

4. **Pure typographic wordmark, no symbol.** No geometric placeholder mark. The wordmark "OCX" set tightly is the brand. Logo follows what Stripe / Vercel / Linear / Cloudflare do.

5. **Cut every label that doesn't earn its place.** Section labels like "EVIDENCE / CONSTRUCTION / SOUNDNESS" become meaningless when overused. Two or three across the whole page; the rest are silent.

6. **Single primary CTA per page.** "Read the paper" is the primary action on the homepage. Other actions are secondary text links, not equal-weight buttons.
